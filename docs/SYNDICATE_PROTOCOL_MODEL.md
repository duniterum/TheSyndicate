## 3. Membership, seat, and contribution doctrine

The membership seat is binary. Contribution is variable.

- A wallet becomes seated by holding SYN.
- SYN is the V1 membership seat.
- The seat is the identity unit, not the USDC amount, purchase count,
  referral count, rank, or transaction count.
- One seated wallet has one seat identity.
- A member entering with 5 USDC and a member entering with 10,000 USDC both
  become seated. The difference is SYN acquired, protocol contribution, USDC
  routed, capital footprint, contribution-depth standing, historical footprint,
  chapter position, and future recognition candidates.

The Syndicate recognizes capital without reducing identity to capital. Capital
footprint is visible and respected as verified USDC routed through receipts, but
it remains one recognition axis among many.

Current seat status, historical identity, and acquisition attribution are
separate layers. Seat truth follows SYN balance, historical identity follows
receipts and Holder Index, and source attribution records introduction under
explicit terms. The canonical boundary is
`docs/IDENTITY_ATTRIBUTION_CONSTITUTION.md`.

Do not collapse member count into economic scale.

- Member count answers: how many seated wallets exist?
- Economic scale answers: how much contribution entered the institution, how
  much USDC routed, how much liquidity formed, how much operational capacity
  exists, how much acquisition cost was spent once such systems are live, and
  how much future revenue is generated by future products or services.

Ranks must never become a wealth leaderboard. Contribution-depth standing may
reflect verified capital footprint, contribution depth, and protocol position,
but it must not imply payout rights, governance rights, yield, ownership, or
financial entitlement.

The institution should not only ask what a member paid. Future recognition,
Builder Records, Chronicle, Register, Archive, and SeatRecord721 thinking may
also ask what the participant helped the institution become. This is
institutional trust capital: builder work, operations, legal/compliance
support, audits, infrastructure, documentation, security, introductions,
preservation, and other historically meaningful contributions.

Referral remains one acquisition layer. It is not the business model, not the
institution, and not a yield system. Any future acquisition cost must be
explained inside the wider business model and must remain PENDING until the
contracts and legal approvals exist.

The protocol and company are distinct, but narratively connected. Major
institutional milestones such as an audit, legal opinion, Swiss AG formation,
major infrastructure activation, strategic partnership, or contract deployment
may become Activity -> Register -> Chronicle -> Archive only when they
materially increase institutional trust, capability, history, or resilience.
Not every invoice, expense, vendor payment, or operational task belongs in the
protocol story.
